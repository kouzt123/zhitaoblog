Abstract
In 2015, China NDRC imposed a $975 million fine to U.S. based semiconductor
Qualcomm Inc. for its abuse in dominance of the modem market. The Chinese antitrust law was
young and a lot of decisions raised further debates by the public. This paper tries to examine the
government decision and provide logical explanations behind each argument, as well as
counterarguments that are not included in the case. The paper concluded that such decision was
reasonable yet questionable, because it involved government interests that would affect equal
judgements.
1.0 Introduction
Qualcomm Incorporated is a San Diego based company focused in building chips that are
made for devices of any kind to connect to the internet. Qualcomm is one of the major patient
holders for CDMA, WCDMA, LTE technologies, commonly known as 2G, 3G, and 4G
networks. These technologies are widely used for people around the world to communicate with
each other. There is no exception in China, seeing that most phones are powered by Qualcomm
modem, the technological term used to describe the wireless internet component. After a 14
months’ investigation from November 2013 to February 2015, the Chinese Government Agency
“National Development and Reform Commission” (NDRC) believed that Qualcomm violated the
antitrust law for its abuse in dominance of the modem market, giving a fine of equivalent 975
million U.S. dollars .
